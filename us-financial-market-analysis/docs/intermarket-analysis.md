# Intermarket Analysis Framework

## Overview

Intermarket analysis is a branch of technical analysis that examines the correlations between four major asset classes: stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies. This methodology, pioneered by John Murphy, recognizes that these markets are interconnected and that shifts in one market often predict changes in others.

## The Four Pillars of Intermarket Analysis

### 1. Stocks vs Bonds
- **Inverse Relationship**: Traditionally, stocks and bonds have an inverse relationship
  - Rising bond prices (falling yields) often coincide with falling stock prices
  - Falling bond prices (rising yields) often precede stock market weakness
- **Leading Indicator**: Bond market trends often precede stock market trends by 3-6 months
- **Interest Rate Impact**: Rising rates hurt both stocks (valuation) and bonds (price)
- **Risk Appetite**: Both reflect investor risk sentiment differently

### 2. Bonds vs Commodities
- **Inflation Expectations**: Rising commodities suggest inflation, hurting bonds
- **Economic Growth**: Strong economy lifts both stocks and commodities, hurts bonds
- **Dollar Impact**: USD strength affects both bonds and commodities inversely

### 3. Commodities vs Currencies
- **Commodity Dollars**: AUD, CAD, NZD, ZAR tied to commodity prices
- **Inflation Hedge**: Commodities vs currency debasement
- **Global Demand**: Reflects international economic health

### 4. Currencies vs Stocks
- **Multinational Impact**: USD strength hurts US multinational profits
- **Capital Flows**: Currency movements reflect investment flows
- **Import/Export**: Currency changes affect corporate earnings

## Key Intermarket Relationships

### Dollar Index (DXY) Relationships
- **Inverse to Commodities**: Strong dollar typically weakens commodity prices
- **Impact on Multinationals**: USD strength reduces overseas earnings
- **Safe Haven Status**: USD rises during risk-off periods

### Yield Curve Dynamics
- **Steepening**: Economic growth expectations, bullish for stocks, bearish for bonds
- **Flattening**: Economic slowdown fears, bearish for banks, bullish for bonds
- **Inversion**: Recession signal, typically bullish for bonds, bearish for stocks

### Risk vs Safety Rotation
- **Risk-On**: USD weakens, commodities rise, stocks advance, bonds decline
- **Risk-Off**: USD strengthens, bonds rally, stocks decline, commodities weaken

## Practical Application

### Leading vs Lagging Analysis
1. Identify which asset class is leading the trend
2. Use leading market to predict direction of lagging markets
3. Look for divergences between correlated assets as potential reversal signals

### Trend Confirmation
- Multiple asset classes confirming trend increases probability
- Divergences between correlated assets signal potential trend changes
- Use strongest performing asset class as benchmark for others

### Sector Rotation
- Technology stocks vs interest rate sensitive sectors
- Cyclical vs defensive sectors based on economic cycle
- Materials/energy vs utilities/consumer staples

## Advanced Concepts

### Cross-Asset Correlation Monitoring
- Dynamic correlation coefficients that change with market regimes
- Correlation breakdowns as early warning signals
- Volatility transmission between markets

### Global Intermarket Analysis
- Developed vs emerging market relationships
- Cross-border capital flow impacts
- Currency intervention effects

### Business Cycle Applications
- Early cycle: Bonds decline, stocks advance, commodities consolidate, USD weakens
- Mid cycle: Stocks peak, bonds stabilize, commodities advance, USD consolidates
- Late cycle: Bonds rally, stocks decline, commodities peak, USD strengthens
- Recession: All risk assets decline, USD and bonds rally

## Warning Signals

### Divergence Signals
- Bond market declining while stocks advance (rate hike concerns)
- Commodities rising rapidly while bonds fall (inflation fears)
- Dollar strengthening while commodities weaken (global demand concerns)
- Yield curve flattening while stocks still advancing (growth concerns)

### Correlation Breakdowns
- Historically correlated assets moving independently
- Safe haven correlations failing during stress periods
- Currency correlations changing due to policy interventions

## Risk Management

### Position Sizing Based on Intermarket Context
- Reduce exposure when multiple asset classes signal risk
- Increase when multiple confirm opportunity
- Adjust based on correlation strength

### Hedging Opportunities
- Use negatively correlated assets for portfolio protection
- Currency hedges for international exposure
- Interest rate hedges for fixed income sensitivity
