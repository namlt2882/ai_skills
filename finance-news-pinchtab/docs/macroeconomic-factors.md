# Macroeconomic Factors for News Context

## Overview

Macroeconomic analysis examines the broad economic forces that drive financial markets. When analyzing financial news, understanding the macroeconomic context helps assess the significance of data releases, policy decisions, and economic trends on different asset classes.

## Key Macroeconomic Indicators

### Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

**Definition**: Total value of goods and services produced in an economy

**Components:**
- Consumption (C) - Consumer spending
- Investment (I) - Business investment
- Government Spending (G) - Public expenditure
- Net Exports (NX) - Exports minus imports

**Market Impact:**

| GDP Signal | Stocks | Bonds | Currency |
|------------|--------|-------|----------|
| Rising GDP | Bullish | Bearish | Bullish |
| Falling GDP | Bearish | Bullish | Bearish |
| GDP Beat | Bullish | Bearish | Bullish |
| GDP Miss | Bearish | Bullish | Bearish |

**News Context:**
- GDP revisions can cause significant market reactions
- Advance > Preliminary > Final releases (increasing accuracy)
- GDP growth rate trends more important than single readings

### Employment Indicators

#### Non-Farm Payrolls (NFP)

**Release**: First Friday of each month

**Market Impact:**

| NFP Signal | USD | Bonds | Stocks |
|------------|-----|-------|--------|
| Strong NFP | Bullish | Bearish | Mixed |
| Weak NFP | Bearish | Bullish | Mixed |
| Beat > 50K | Bullish | Bearish | Risk-on |
| Miss < 50K | Bearish | Bullish | Risk-off |

**News Context:**
- Most market-moving US data release
- Revisions to prior months are important
- Wage growth component affects inflation expectations

#### Unemployment Rate

**Characteristics:**
- Lagging indicator (changes after economic conditions shift)
- Influences Fed policy decisions
- Affects consumer spending expectations

**News Context:**
- Lower unemployment = wage inflation concerns
- Higher unemployment = recession fears
- Participation rate changes affect interpretation

#### Job Openings and Labor Turnover (JOLTS)

**Characteristics:**
- Leading indicator for labor market
- Job openings signal wage inflation pressure
- Quit rate indicates worker confidence

**News Context:**
- High job openings = tight labor market
- Rising quit rate = worker confidence
- JOLTS trends precede NFP changes

### Inflation Indicators

#### Consumer Price Index (CPI)

**Release**: Mid-month, with core CPI (excluding food and energy)

**Market Impact:**

| CPI Signal | Bonds | USD | Stocks |
|------------|-------|-----|--------|
| Higher than expected | Bearish | Bullish | Mixed |
| Lower than expected | Bullish | Bearish | Bullish |
| Core rising | Bearish | Bullish | Bearish |

**News Context:**
- Primary inflation metric for markets
- Core CPI more important for Fed policy
- Shelter component has large weight

#### Producer Price Index (PPI)

**Characteristics:**
- Leading indicator for consumer inflation
- Measures inflation at producer level
- Affects corporate margin expectations

**News Context:**
- Rising PPI = margin pressure for companies
- PPI trends often precede CPI changes
- Input cost inflation signals

#### Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE)

**Characteristics:**
- Fed's preferred inflation measure
- Core PCE excludes food and energy
- Directly influences Fed decisions

**News Context:**
- More important for Fed than CPI
- PCE divergence from CPI is significant
- Fed targets 2% PCE inflation

### Manufacturing and Services Indicators

#### Purchasing Managers' Index (PMI)

**Interpretation:**
- PMI > 50 = Expansion
- PMI < 50 = Contraction
- PMI > 60 = Strong expansion
- PMI < 40 = Severe contraction

**Market Impact:**

| PMI Signal | Stocks | Currency | Bonds |
|------------|--------|----------|-------|
| Rising PMI | Bullish | Bullish | Bearish |
| Falling PMI | Bearish | Bearish | Bullish |
| PMI > 50 | Bullish | Bullish | Bearish |
| PMI < 50 | Bearish | Bearish | Bullish |

**News Context:**
- Leading indicator for GDP
- Manufacturing PMI for industrial economy
- Services PMI for modern economy (larger component)

#### Industrial Production

**Characteristics:**
- Measures output of factories, mines, utilities
- Capacity utilization shows economic slack
- Indicates manufacturing health

**News Context:**
- Capacity utilization > 85% = inflationary pressure
- Rising production = economic growth
- Sector breakdown shows industry trends

## Federal Reserve Policy

### Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC)

**Meeting Schedule**: 8 times per year

**Key Outputs:**
- Policy statement
- Interest rate decision
- Economic projections (SEP)
- Dot plot (rate projections)
- Press conference

**Market Impact:**

| Fed Signal | Stocks | Bonds | USD |
|------------|--------|-------|-----|
| Rate hike | Mixed | Bearish | Bullish |
| Rate cut | Bullish | Bullish | Bearish |
| Hawkish tone | Bearish | Bearish | Bullish |
| Dovish tone | Bullish | Bullish | Bearish |

**News Context:**
- Statement language changes are significant
- Dot plot shifts affect rate expectations
- Press conference can cause volatility

### Interest Rate Decisions

**Federal Funds Rate**: Primary monetary policy tool

**Rate Cycle Impact:**

| Cycle Phase | Rates | Stocks | Bonds | Economy |
|-------------|-------|--------|-------|---------|
| Early hiking | Rising | Mixed | Falling | Strong |
| Late hiking | High | Falling | Falling | Slowing |
| Early cutting | Falling | Rising | Rising | Weak |
| Late cutting | Low | Rising | Rising | Recovering |

**News Context:**
- Market prices in expected rate changes
- Surprise decisions cause major moves
- Forward guidance as important as decisions

### Quantitative Easing (QE) and Tightening (QT)

**QE Impact:**
- Increases money supply
- Lowers interest rates
- Bullish for risk assets
- Weakens currency

**QT Impact:**
- Reduces money supply
- Raises interest rates
- Bearish for risk assets
- Strengthens currency

**News Context:**
- Balance sheet announcements significant
- QE/QT pace changes affect markets
- Communication about future policy important

## Practical Application

### Pre-Data Assessment

Before major data releases:

1. **Check consensus expectations**
   - What is priced in?
   - Range of estimates

2. **Review recent trends**
   - Last 3-6 months of data
   - Revisions to prior data

3. **Assess market positioning**
   - How are markets positioned?
   - What would be a surprise?

### Post-Data Analysis

After data release:

1. **Compare to expectations**
   - Beat/miss magnitude
   - Revisions to prior

2. **Analyze components**
   - What drove the headline?
   - Any divergences within?

3. **Assess policy implications**
   - How does this affect Fed expectations?
   - What does it mean for rates?

4. **Update macro view**
   - Does this change the outlook?
   - What to watch next?
